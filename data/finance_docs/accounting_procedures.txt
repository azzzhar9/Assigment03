Accounting Procedures and Financial Controls

Introduction:
This document outlines the accounting procedures, internal controls, and financial processes that all employees must follow to ensure accurate financial reporting, compliance with regulations, and protection of company assets.

General Accounting Principles:

Fundamental Concepts:
The company follows Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) for all financial reporting and transactions. Key principles include:
- Accrual basis accounting (revenue and expenses recorded when earned/incurred)
- Matching principle (expenses matched to related revenues)
- Conservatism (recognize losses immediately, gains when realized)
- Consistency (use same methods year over year)
- Materiality (focus on significant items)

Accounting Period:
- Fiscal year: January 1 through December 31
- Monthly closing: Books close on last day of each month
- Quarterly reporting: March 31, June 30, September 30, December 31
- Annual audit: Completed by March 31 of following year

Chart of Accounts:

Account Structure:
The company uses a standardized chart of accounts with the following structure:
- 1000-1999: Assets
- 2000-2999: Liabilities
- 3000-3999: Equity
- 4000-4999: Revenue
- 5000-5999: Cost of Goods Sold
- 6000-6999: Operating Expenses
- 7000-7999: Other Income/Expense

Account Numbering:
Each account number contains 4 digits plus optional department code:
- Format: XXXX-YYY (account-department)
- Example: 6100-150 (Office Supplies - Marketing Department)
- Department codes standardized across all accounts

Creating New Accounts:
New accounts require:
- Business justification
- Controller approval
- Documentation in account master
- Training for affected users
- Update to COA documentation

Accounts Payable:

Invoice Processing:

Receipt and Review:
1. Vendor invoices received centrally by AP team
2. Invoices matched to purchase orders (3-way match)
3. Verify pricing, quantities, and terms
4. Check for duplicate invoices
5. Ensure proper account coding
6. Obtain approval from department manager
7. Enter into accounting system

Invoice Coding:
All invoices must include:
- Vendor name and invoice number
- Invoice date and due date
- General ledger account code
- Department or cost center
- Amount and payment terms
- Approver signature or electronic approval

Invoice Approval Limits:
- Up to $1,000: Department supervisor
- $1,001 - $5,000: Department manager
- $5,001 - $25,000: Director
- $25,001 - $100,000: Vice President
- Over $100,000: CFO or CEO

Payment Processing:

Payment Schedule:
Invoices are processed according to payment terms:
- Net 30: Paid within 30 days of invoice date
- Net 15: Paid within 15 days
- 2/10 Net 30: Paid within 10 days to capture discount
- Due upon receipt: Paid immediately
- Payment runs executed weekly on Wednesdays

Payment Methods:
- ACH/Wire Transfer: For amounts over $10,000 or international payments
- Check: For amounts under $10,000 and domestic vendors
- Credit Card: For small purchases and subscriptions under $5,000
- Electronic payment portals: When required by vendor

Payment Authorization:
All payments require dual authorization:
- First approval: Department manager (invoice approval)
- Second approval: AP manager or Controller (payment release)
- Segregation of duties enforced
- Exception reports reviewed weekly

Vendor Management:

Vendor Setup:
New vendors require:
- Completed W-9 form (for tax reporting)
- Vendor application with contact and banking information
- Certificate of Insurance if applicable
- Vendor agreement or contract
- Credit check for significant relationships
- Approval from Procurement and Finance

Vendor Master Maintenance:
- Annual review of active vendors
- Update contact and payment information
- Verify tax ID numbers
- Remove inactive vendors (no activity 2+ years)
- Monitor for duplicate vendors

Vendor Reconciliation:
Monthly vendor statement reconciliation:
- Compare vendor statements to AP aging
- Investigate and resolve discrepancies
- Clear unapplied payments
- Follow up on outstanding invoices
- Document reconciliation in vendor file

1099 Reporting:
For tax compliance:
- Track all payments to contractors and service providers
- Verify W-9 information annually
- Generate 1099 forms by January 31
- File with IRS and state agencies
- Distribute to vendors by required dates

Accounts Receivable:

Invoicing Procedures:

Invoice Generation:
1. Sales order approved and fulfilled
2. Shipping documentation confirmed
3. Invoice generated from billing system
4. Review for accuracy (pricing, terms, customer info)
5. Attach supporting documentation (contracts, POs)
6. Deliver invoice to customer (email or mail)
7. Record in accounting system

Invoice Requirements:
All customer invoices must include:
- Company name and logo
- Invoice number (sequential)
- Invoice date
- Customer name and billing address
- Payment terms and due date
- Itemized products/services
- Quantities, prices, and extensions
- Applicable taxes
- Total amount due
- Payment instructions (wire, check, credit card)

Payment Terms:
Standard payment terms by customer type:
- Government: Net 30 or per contract
- Enterprise customers: Net 30
- Small business: Net 15
- New customers: Prepayment or Net 15
- International: Wire transfer in advance
- Special terms require CFO approval

Collections:

Collection Process:
Systematic approach to collecting receivables:
- Day 0: Invoice sent to customer
- Day 15: Reminder email for Net 30 terms
- Day 31: First collection call
- Day 45: Second collection call and email
- Day 60: Final notice before escalation
- Day 75: Executive escalation
- Day 90: Legal action or collections agency

Past Due Management:
For overdue accounts:
- Apply late fees per terms (1.5% per month)
- Place account on credit hold (no new orders)
- Escalate to sales manager
- Offer payment plans if appropriate
- Consider factoring or collections agency
- Write off if deemed uncollectible

Customer Communication:
Collection calls should:
- Be professional and courteous
- Verify invoice received and approved
- Identify any disputes or issues
- Obtain commitment to pay with specific date
- Document all conversations
- Follow up in writing

Credit Management:

Credit Application:
New customers complete credit application:
- Business references
- Bank references
- Trade references
- Financial statements (if applicable)
- Personal guarantee for small businesses
- Credit check through credit bureau

Credit Limits:
Establish credit limits based on:
- Credit score and payment history
- Financial strength and stability
- Years in business
- Industry and market conditions
- Relationship and sales history
- Risk tolerance

Credit limits reviewed annually and adjusted as needed.

Credit Hold:
Accounts placed on hold when:
- Payment 60+ days past due
- Exceeds credit limit
- Check returned for insufficient funds
- Repeated payment issues
- Bankruptcy or financial distress
Release requires payment and manager approval.

General Ledger:

Journal Entries:

Standard Journal Entries:
Recurring monthly entries include:
- Depreciation expense
- Accrued expenses (payroll, benefits, utilities)
- Prepaid amortization (insurance, rent)
- Accrued revenue
- Deferred revenue
- Inventory adjustments

Entry Requirements:
All journal entries must include:
- Entry number (auto-generated)
- Date (posting date)
- Description explaining purpose
- Account numbers and descriptions
- Debit and credit amounts (must balance)
- Supporting documentation attached
- Preparer name and date
- Reviewer approval

Entry Approval:
- Standard recurring entries: Pre-approved template
- Monthly accruals under $10,000: Accounting Manager
- One-time entries under $25,000: Controller
- Entries over $25,000: CFO approval
- Year-end entries: CFO and external auditor review

Account Reconciliations:

Required Reconciliations:
Monthly reconciliation required for:
- All balance sheet accounts
- Bank and credit card accounts
- Intercompany accounts
- Significant revenue and expense accounts
- Inventory and fixed assets
- Payroll and benefits

Reconciliation Process:
1. Download or prepare account detail
2. Compare to supporting documentation (bank statements, sub-ledgers)
3. Identify and investigate differences
4. Prepare reconciling items and adjustments
5. Document explanation for variances
6. Obtain manager review and approval
7. File reconciliation in monthly close folder
8. Prepare adjustment entries if needed

Reconciliation Timing:
- Bank accounts: Within 5 business days of month end
- Credit cards: Within 7 business days
- Other balance sheet: Within 10 business days
- Revenue and expense: Within 15 business days
- Annual reconciliations: All accounts by January 31

Fixed Assets:

Asset Capitalization:

Capitalization Policy:
Assets are capitalized if they meet all criteria:
- Purchased or constructed for business use
- Useful life exceeds one year
- Cost exceeds $5,000 per unit
- Not intended for resale
- Provides future economic benefit

Asset Categories:
- Land: Not depreciated
- Buildings: 39 years straight-line
- Leasehold improvements: Lesser of useful life or lease term
- Machinery and equipment: 5-7 years
- Computers and technology: 3-5 years
- Furniture and fixtures: 7 years
- Vehicles: 5 years

Depreciation Methods:
- Buildings and improvements: Straight-line
- Equipment and machinery: Straight-line or accelerated
- Vehicles: Straight-line
- Technology: Straight-line (shorter useful lives)

Asset Management:

Asset Tracking:
Maintain fixed asset register with:
- Asset tag number (physical tag attached)
- Description and category
- Location and responsible employee
- Purchase date and vendor
- Original cost and useful life
- Accumulated depreciation
- Net book value
- Warranty and maintenance information

Annual Physical Inventory:
- Conduct annual fixed asset verification
- Compare physical count to asset register
- Investigate missing assets
- Update locations and custodians
- Write off fully depreciated assets no longer in use
- Adjust register for disposals and scrapping

Asset Disposal:
Disposing of assets requires:
- Disposal request form completed
- Management approval
- Removal from asset register
- Calculation of gain or loss
- Certificate of destruction if required
- Update insurance records

Inventory Accounting:

Inventory Valuation:

Valuation Method:
Company uses weighted average cost method:
- Calculate average cost per unit
- Include purchase price plus freight and duties
- Exclude sales tax
- Recompute average with each purchase
- Value ending inventory at average cost

Inventory Categories:
- Raw materials: Components and supplies
- Work in process: Partially completed goods
- Finished goods: Completed products ready for sale
- Supplies: Indirect materials and consumables

Lower of Cost or Market:
- Review inventory quarterly for obsolescence
- Write down to market value if lower than cost
- Consider age, condition, and salability
- Document write-down justification
- Obtain management approval for material write-offs

Inventory Controls:

Physical Inventory:
- Annual full physical count (December 31)
- Cycle counting throughout year (ABC classification)
- Reconcile counts to perpetual records
- Investigate and resolve variances
- Adjust inventory records
- Report results to management

Inventory Procedures:
- Receiving: Count and inspect upon receipt
- Storage: Organize by category, first-in-first-out (FIFO)
- Picking: Use pick lists, verify quantities
- Shipping: Pack and verify against sales orders
- Returns: Inspect and return to inventory if saleable

Shrinkage and Loss:
- Calculate shrinkage (physical vs. perpetual)
- Investigate causes (theft, damage, errors)
- Implement controls to reduce shrinkage
- Write off obsolete or damaged inventory
- Maintain acceptable shrinkage rate (<2%)

Month-End Close Process:

Close Timeline:

Day 1-3 (Business Days After Month End):
- Reconcile cash and credit card accounts
- Review and approve pending transactions
- Process month-end payroll accruals
- Record depreciation expense
- Post standard monthly journal entries

Day 4-7:
- Complete all account reconciliations
- Review significant revenue and expense variances
- Prepare accrual and prepayment entries
- Reconcile intercompany accounts
- Close subledgers (AR, AP, inventory, fixed assets)

Day 8-10:
- Management review of financial results
- Finalize adjusting journal entries
- Prepare financial statements (balance sheet, income statement, cash flow)
- Complete variance analysis vs. budget
- Package close documentation

Close Checklist:
Accounting Manager maintains checklist confirming:
- All bank accounts reconciled
- All balance sheet accounts reconciled
- All journal entries posted and approved
- All subledgers closed and reconciled
- Financial statements prepared
- Variances explained
- Documentation complete
- Close summary presented to CFO

Financial Reporting:

Monthly Financial Statements:

Statement Package:
Complete financial package includes:
- Balance Sheet (assets, liabilities, equity)
- Income Statement (revenue and expenses by department)
- Cash Flow Statement (operating, investing, financing)
- Budget vs. Actual with variance analysis
- Key performance indicators (KPIs)
- Management commentary and explanations

Distribution:
- CFO: Day 10 after month end
- CEO and Executive Team: Day 12
- Board of Directors: Quarterly
- Department Heads: Limited package Day 15
- External parties: Only with CFO approval

Variance Analysis:
Explain significant variances:
- Revenue variances over $50,000 or 10%
- Expense variances over $25,000 or 10%
- Identify one-time vs. recurring items
- Provide trend analysis
- Forecast impact on year-end results

Financial Analysis:

Key Metrics:
Monitor critical financial metrics:
- Revenue growth rate
- Gross profit margin
- Operating expense ratio
- EBITDA and net profit margin
- Days sales outstanding (DSO)
- Inventory turnover
- Current ratio and quick ratio
- Debt-to-equity ratio
- Return on assets (ROA)
- Return on equity (ROE)

Trend Analysis:
- Month-over-month comparison
- Year-over-year comparison
- Rolling 12-month trends
- Seasonality adjustments
- Industry benchmarking

Forecasting:
- Monthly reforecast of year-end results
- Quarterly forecast updates
- Three-year strategic financial plan
- Scenario analysis (best/worst/expected)

Internal Controls:

Segregation of Duties:

Key Segregations:
- Authorization vs. execution
- Record keeping vs. custody
- Reconciliation vs. transaction processing
- Different people for: Ordering, receiving, approving, paying

Examples:
- Person approving invoices doesn't process payments
- Person receiving goods doesn't enter receiving records
- Person reconciling accounts doesn't process transactions
- Person with custody of assets doesn't maintain records

Compensating Controls:
When segregation not possible:
- Enhanced management oversight
- Detailed transaction review
- Automated system controls
- Regular external reviews

Authorization Controls:

Approval Matrix:
- Capital expenditures: Per capital budget policy
- Operating expenses: Per budget approval limits
- Contracts: Based on value and term
- Write-offs: Controller approval required
- Journal entries: Based on amount and type

Documentation Requirements:
- All transactions supported by documentation
- Approvals documented and traceable
- Retention per record retention policy
- Electronic approvals via workflow system
- Manual approvals scanned and filed

Access Controls:

System Access:
- Role-based access (least privilege)
- Segregation enforced in system
- Periodic access reviews (quarterly)
- Terminated employee access removed immediately
- Vendor and consultant access temporary

Audit:

Internal Audit:
- Annual internal control assessment
- Testing of key controls
- Process improvement recommendations
- Follow-up on prior findings
- Report to Audit Committee

External Audit:
- Annual financial statement audit
- Interim reviews and agreed-upon procedures
- Management letter with recommendations
- Audit report and opinion
- Audit findings resolution
